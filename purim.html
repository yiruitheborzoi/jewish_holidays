Purim

Purim is a joyous festival that commemorates the salvation of the Jewish people from Haman’s plot in ancient Persia, as told in the Book of Esther. It usually falls in late winter or early spring.

Overview

Purim recalls how Queen Esther and her cousin Mordecai foiled the wicked Haman’s plan to annihilate the Jews of Persia. The name Purim derives from the lots (pur) that Haman cast to determine the date of destruction.

Customs & Traditions

The Megillah (Scroll of Esther) is read aloud, and listeners boo at Haman’s name. Festive meals are held, often with costumes and plays reenacting the story. Gifts of food (mishloach manot) are exchanged, and donations are given to those in need (matanot la’evyonim). Triangular pastries called hamantaschen are enjoyed.

Significance

Purim celebrates the reversal of fortune and survival against all odds. Its message underscores unity, courage and the importance of standing up against injustice while encouraging generosity and joy.
