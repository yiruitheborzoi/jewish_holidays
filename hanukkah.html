Hanukkah

Hanukkah, the Festival of Lights, commemorates the Maccabean revolt and the rededication of the Second Temple. It begins on the 25th of Kislev and lasts eight nights.

Overview

Hanukkah celebrates the victory of the Maccabees over the Syrian–Greek empire and the miracle of a small jar of oil that burned for eight days. It is a rabbinic holiday rather than one commanded in the Torah.

Customs & Traditions

Each night a candle or oil lamp is added to a nine‑branched menorah, using the helper candle (shammash) to light the others. Families sing songs, recite blessings and display the menorah in a window. Games of dreidel are played, and foods fried in oil—such as latkes and sufganiyot—are enjoyed. Giving small gifts has become a modern custom.

Significance

Hanukkah honors religious freedom and perseverance. The miracle of the oil symbolizes hope and faith in the face of adversity, inspiring people to bring light into dark times.
