Passover

Passover (Pesach) commemorates the Israelites’ liberation from slavery in Egypt. The eight-day spring festival begins on the 15th of Nisan and centers around family and community gatherings.

Overview

This festival recalls the Exodus story when God “passed over” the homes of the Israelites during the tenth plague. It is one of the three pilgrimage festivals and lasts seven days in Israel and eight days in the Diaspora.

Customs & Traditions

Before the holiday, homes are cleansed of chametz (leaven). The first two nights are marked by a seder—a ritual meal that follows the Haggadah, a text recounting the Exodus. Participants eat symbolic foods like matzah (unleavened bread), maror (bitter herbs), roasted bone and charoset (fruit and nut mixture), and children ask the Four Questions. Work is restricted on the first and last days.

Significance

Passover celebrates freedom and deliverance from oppression. It encourages participants to empathize with those still enslaved and to renew commitments to justice, hospitality and gratitude for liberation.
