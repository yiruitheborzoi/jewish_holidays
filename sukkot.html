Sukkot

Sukkot is a week‑long harvest festival that begins on the 15th of Tishri. It honors the bounty of the autumn harvest and commemorates the fragile shelters used by the Israelites during their journey through the wilderness.

Overview

One of the three pilgrimage festivals, Sukkot lasts seven days and invites Jews to celebrate the harvest while recalling the temporary dwellings built by their ancestors. It begins five days after Yom Kippur and is often called the Feast of Booths or Tabernacles.

Customs & Traditions

Families build a sukkah, a simple hut with at least three walls and a roof of branches. Meals are enjoyed inside, and some people sleep there to experience the impermanence of life. A key ritual involves waving the four species — palm frond, myrtle, willow and citron — in six directions, acknowledging God’s presence everywhere.

Significance

The festival expresses gratitude for the autumn harvest and reminds Jews of their dependence on divine protection in the wilderness. Dwelling in a sukkah encourages humility, a connection to nature and trust in something greater than oneself.
