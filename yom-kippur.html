Yom Kippur

The Day of Atonement is considered the most solemn and holiest day of the Jewish year. Falling on the 10th day of Tishri, it brings to a close the Ten Days of Repentance that begin with Rosh Hashanah and offers a final opportunity to seek forgiveness and set intentions for the year ahead.

Overview

Yom Kippur is a day of fasting, prayer and repentance. It is devoted to self‑examination and supplication: congregants gather in synagogues to confess misdeeds, recite liturgical poems and ask for forgiveness from God and from one another. The holiday’s central purpose is spiritual purification through forgiveness, contrition and resolve to do good in the coming year.

Customs & Observance

On this solemn day, observers abstain from food and drink for roughly twenty‑five hours and refrain from work, bathing, wearing leather shoes and other physical comforts. The holiday opens with the haunting chant of Kol Nidre on the eve of Yom Kippur and continues with day‑long services featuring repeated confessional prayers and readings, including the Book of Jonah. Many wear white garments to symbolize purity and humility, and the fast concludes at nightfall with a joyous burst of the shofar.

Significance

Yom Kippur offers individuals and communities a chance to acknowledge misdeeds, atone for past wrongs and reset their spiritual course. The day embodies themes of forgiveness and renewal, reminding worshippers that sincere repentance and compassionate deeds can alter the divine decree. By fasting and praying together, Jews hope to be sealed in the Book of Life for a year of peace and blessing.
